DMR Radio Basics Lesson 02

How a DMR Transmission Travels

From PTT to the far ear, DMR voice can take three different roads. Knowing which road tells you exactly where your on-device bridge plugs in.

In Lesson 1 you learned the four fields that define a channel. Now follow the voice itself. The same handset can reach the other person three ways — and the reach mode decides whether the internet is even involved, which matters a great deal for a device that can also speak PoC.

The voice path, inside the radio

Every DMR transmission starts the same way: your voice is sampled, then squeezed by the vocoder (DVSI AMBE+2) down to roughly 3,600 bps of digital data.1 That tiny compressed stream — not raw audio — is what flies over the air in one time slot. At the far radio, the vocoder runs in reverse to reconstruct speech.

Hold onto this for the bridge The thing crossing the air is AMBE+2 frames. On your Android radio, the central question is what the DMR module hands your app at the bridge point: decoded PCM audio (you then re-encode for PoC — a transcode) or the raw AMBE frames (you could relay them untouched). That one fact decides whether bridging costs voice quality. We dedicate Lesson 4 to it.

Three roads the transmission can take

1 · Direct / simplex — no infrastructure

Two radios on the same frequency, time slot and color code talk straight to each other, radio-to-radio. No repeater, no internet. Range is line-of-sight.2 This is DMR Tier I-style operation.

Connect that repeater (or a personal hotspot) to a DMR network like Brandmeister or DMR-MARC over the internet, and a talkgroup can be routed to matching repeaters anywhere on Earth.4 The talkgroup is the address that tells the network who should receive the voice.

The pattern As you move down the list, you trade simplicity for reach: direct (no infra) → repeater (local infra) → network (internet, global). Only road 3 already uses an IP network end-to-end.

Where your on-device bridge taps in

Here is the twist for your product. A normal DMR user needs road 3 — a repeater plus an internet-linked network — to reach beyond the horizon. Your radio already has an IP path of its own: the PoC stack. So the bridge can take a transmission off the DMR side (any of the three roads above) and carry it onward over PoC instead — and vice-versa — all inside one device.

Your device effectively becomes the "network" for the DMR side — but the transport is your PoC platform, not Brandmeister. The bridge replaces road 3's infrastructure with cellular/IP, and it reads the same routing metadata (talkgroup, slot, color code) to know where the voice belongs.
