DMR Radio Basics Lesson 08

How a Radio Talks to a Repeater

From PTT press to release, step by step. The headline: in conventional Tier II there is no handshake — it's listen, sync, and transmit a structured stream.

You asked for every step of a device talking to a repeater. The first thing to unlearn is the word "connection." Conventional DMR Tier II has no TCP-style SYN/ACK, no login, no channel-grant. The repeater never says "go ahead." What looks like a handshake is two simpler things: the radio one-way synchronising to the repeater's clock, and a quick politeness check before it keys.1

The one big idea The repeater's outbound transmission is the timing master; the radio follows it. Access is gated by a color-code + slot-free check the radio performs — not by permission the repeater grants. (Trunked Tier III does add real registration and channel grants — don't conflate the two.)

Before anyone keys up: staying in sync

An active repeater transmits continuously on its outbound frequency, even with no call up — sending IDLE bursts with embedded sync patterns and the CACH. A radio decodes this to lock onto where slot 1 and slot 2 begin (each slot 30 ms; a frame of both = 60 ms). This is the channel-timing relationship the standard defines in TS 102 361-1, Clause 4.4.1 The uplink (radio→repeater) carries no CACH — the radio puts a guard period there instead, to absorb timing differences between near and far radios.

CACH — how the radio knows a slot is free

The CACH (Common Announcement Channel) rides on the repeater's outbound stream, interleaved between the two slots. It tells a listening radio, without decoding any voice: is this slot busy or idle (AT), is it slot 1 or 2 (TC), and carries fragments of a Short LC (LCSS).1 That's what lets a polite radio decide instantly whether it may transmit.

The PTT sequence — press to release

  1. PTT pressed → channel-access check. TS 102 361-1, Clause 5.2
    The radio applies its admit criterion against what it hears on the downlink: Always (impolite — key regardless), Channel Free (only if the slot is idle), or Color Code Free (only if the slot is idle on the repeater matching my color code — the recommended setting). The color code here is a filter to reject co-channel repeaters, not an address.2
  2. (Optional) Preamble CSBK.
    On networked/MMDVM systems the radio may send one or more Control Signalling Blocks first — used to wake the system and help scanning/late-entry radios. Formal repeater activation messaging is defined in TS 102 361-2, Clause 4.2.3
  3. Voice LC Header — sent before any voice.
    A Link Control PDU announcing the call: source ID (24-bit), destination (talkgroup or Radio ID), call type (the FLCO opcode: group vs individual), and FID (feature/manufacturer ID). This is what lets every receiving radio decide "is this for me?" and lock on.
  4. Voice superframes — the actual speech.
    Voice flows as superframes of 6 bursts, A–F (360 ms). Burst A carries the voice SYNC; bursts B–F replace sync with embedded signalling carrying the Link Control again, fragmented. Each burst's payload = three 20 ms AMBE+2 vocoder frames = 60 ms of speech.
  5. PTT released → Terminator with LC.
    The radio sends a terminator burst (its own sync pattern) carrying the full Link Control once more, cleanly closing the call.
  6. Hang time.
    The repeater keeps that slot + talkgroup reserved for a second or two so the other party can reply without the slot being grabbed for a different talkgroup. A radio on a different talkgroup hears "busy"; one on the same talkgroup may key straight in. Hang time is per-slot.

Inside one burst (264 bits, 27.5 ms)

Every transmission above is a stream of these. A burst is two 108-bit payload halves around a 48-bit centre. The centre is the clever part: it's either a SYNC pattern or embedded signalling (a 16-bit EMB + a 32-bit LC fragment).

Why the LC is repeated: late entry

Because bursts B–F re-send the Link Control every superframe, a radio that joins mid-transmission — missing the header — still recovers the source, talkgroup and call type within one superframe (≤360 ms). That's late entry, and it's why scanning radios can announce who's talking after tuning in late.

What this means for your bridge Your DMR module handles all of this air-interface choreography for you — you won't be timing bursts. But three of these elements surface directly at your bridge boundary:
  • The Voice LC Header / embedded LC is where source ID, talkgroup and call type come from — the exact fields you map to PoC groups/users. Expect the module's API to expose these as call-start metadata.
  • Late entry means a DMR call's identity is knowable even if you start listening mid-stream — useful if your bridge attaches to an in-progress call.
  • Hang time is a real behaviour to mirror: after a PoC→DMR transmission, hold the talkgroup briefly so the DMR reply lands on the same conversation. Don't tear down instantly.
