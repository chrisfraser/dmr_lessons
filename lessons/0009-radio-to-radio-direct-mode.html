DMR Radio Basics Lesson 09

Radio to Radio (Direct Mode)

Take the repeater away and one thing changes everything: with no continuous carrier to keep time, the transmitting radio becomes the clock. Everything else follows from that.

In Lesson 8 the repeater was the timing master and the radios followed it. Direct Mode (DMO) — two radios talking simplex on one frequency, no infrastructure — removes that master. The burst and call structure are identical; what changes is who keeps time, how a radio senses the channel, and how many conversations fit. Get those three deltas and you understand simplex.

The one big idea No repeater means no continuous downlink to pre-synchronise against. So the transmitting radio is the timing reference; a receiver locks on, cold, from the sync pattern in the very first burst of the transmission.1

The three things that change

1 · Timing master shifts to the transmitter

With a repeater, you sync to its always-on carrier before you ever key up. In direct mode there's nothing on the air between calls — so radios transmit asynchronously, and any radio in range syncs to that transmission to receive it.1 The standard guarantees this works by requiring the first burst to carry a sync pattern so a cold receiver can detect the signal and find the burst centre. A preamble (CSBK) often precedes the header to give far radios extra time to wake — this matters more in DMO precisely because there's no carrier keeping receivers warm.

2 · No CACH — sense the channel directly

Recall the CACH rode on the repeater's outbound stream and told radios "slot busy/idle." Simplex has no outbound stream, so there is no CACH and no busy/idle bit to consult.2 A radio instead falls back to plain RF carrier / sync sensing — listen for on-channel DMR activity before keying. That's why the recommended admit criterion for simplex is Channel Free (not Color Code Free, which needs a repeater to poll).3

3 · One conversation, not two — unless DCDM

Without a master coordinating two slots, basic simplex effectively carries one conversation — the transmitter sends bursts at roughly 50% duty and the "second slot" simply goes unmanaged. A receiving radio is slot-agnostic: it accepts the call from either slot.4 The two-slot magic from Lesson 1 needs infrastructure; on bare simplex it isn't there.

Careful with the shorthand People say "simplex uses only one timeslot." More precisely: there is no synchronised two-slot structure at all because no master defines it. One conversation's worth of airtime, the other slot idle. Both phrasings describe the same reality.

Dual Capacity Direct Mode (DCDM) — both slots, still no repeater

DCDM is the exception that gets two simultaneous conversations on one simplex frequency. The radios elect a Channel Timing Leader (by a Preferred / Eligible / Ineligible setting — ideally a stationary, powered radio), and the leader synthesises the repeater's missing timekeeping by broadcasting a timing beacon (~600 ms every 4.5 min) on the "All Site" color code 15, re-radiated to distant radios via "forced propagations."5 Caveats: it's a 2nd-gen-radio feature, needs every participant to support it, and is rarely seen in the wild — fragile in mixed fleets.

What stays exactly the same

  • Burst structure — same 264-bit burst (108 + 48-centre + 108).
  • Call skeleton — the originating radio emits it directly: Voice LC Header → superframes (A–F, 360 ms) → Terminator with LC. No repeater re-radiates it.
  • Color code — still used; both radios must match (CC15 is reserved for DCDM beacons).3
  • Late entry — LC repeated in B–F means a late joiner still recovers source/talkgroup/call type within one superframe.

One tell: who sent the burst

The 48-bit sync words differ for MS-sourced (a radio) versus BS-sourced (a repeater) bursts. A direct-mode radio transmits the MS-sourced pattern, so a decoder can tell direct-mode traffic from repeater traffic by the sync word alone.2

Related: talkaround & simplex hotspots

Talkaround is simplex done on a repeater's output frequency to bypass it when it's down or out of range — TX moved to equal RX.6 A simplex hotspot is single-frequency, so it too runs DMO and handles one slot at a time (convention: CC1, Channel Free); a duplex hotspot acts like a mini-repeater and exposes both slots.

The cold-sync timeline

Compare this with the repeater timeline in Lesson 8: the same skeleton, but Radio A emits it directly and Radio B has to lock on from nothing — no carrier was there a moment ago.

Radio A (TX · timing master)
Radio B (RX)

Every arrow is Radio A transmitting; Radio B only ever receives. There is no second lane of infrastructure — and crucially, no pre-existing carrier, so B's first job is to lock onto A's opening sync pattern.

What this means for your bridge Your radios are Android + DMR module, so a device may be bridging a direct-mode DMR call to PoC — no repeater involved. Implications:
  • Channel sensing is the device's job: with no CACH, "is the channel busy" comes from RF sensing in the module — your PoC→DMR side should respect Channel-Free admit so it doesn't stomp an active simplex QSO.
  • Same call metadata: the Voice LC Header still carries source ID, talkgroup and call type — your TG⇄PoC-group and RadioID⇄user maps work identically to the repeater case.
  • Slot-agnostic RX: don't assume a fixed slot for incoming simplex; the module accepts either. Don't key your mapping logic on slot for direct mode.
  • Range is the reason to bridge: simplex is line-of-sight only — bridging it onto PoC is exactly how your product extends a direct call beyond the horizon, doing what a repeater+network would have.
