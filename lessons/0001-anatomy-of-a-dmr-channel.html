DMR Radio Basics Lesson 01

The Anatomy of a DMR Channel

Four settings define every DMR conversation. Read them, and you can read the radio — and know exactly what your bridge has to honour.

Newcomers picture a radio "channel" as a frequency — one dial, one conversation. DMR breaks that picture. On DMR, a single channel is a bundle of four settings, and a single frequency carries two conversations at once. Get this one idea straight and most of DMR falls into place.

One frequency, two conversations

DMR uses TDMA — it chops the frequency into two repeating 30 ms turns called time slots, TS1 and TS2. Two completely separate groups can talk on the same frequency at the same time, each owning one slot.1 That is why DMR doubles the capacity of a 12.5 kHz analog channel.2

Time Slot 1Group A talking — e.g. wide-area talkgroup
Time Slot 2Group B talking — e.g. local talkgroup

Same frequency. Same instant. Two independent conversations, interleaved 30 ms at a time.

The four fields of a channel

When an operator turns the knob to a channel, the radio loads all four of these together. Miss one and you either can't be heard, or you're heard by the wrong people.

Channel: "Town Repeater — Local"
RX frequency439.4000 MHz  — what you listen on
TX frequency431.4000 MHz  — what you transmit on (offset, for the repeater)
Time slotTS2  — which of the two turns you use
Color code1  — must match the repeater to get in
TalkgroupTG 235  — which virtual group you join

What each one does

RX / TX frequency. Through a repeater, you listen and transmit on different frequencies (a duplex offset); the repeater hears you on one and re-broadcasts on the other to extend range.2

Time slot (TS1 / TS2). Which of the two conversations you join. The repeater's operator decides what each slot is for — a common convention is wide-area/worldwide talkgroups on TS1, local/regional on TS2.3 Pick the wrong slot and you transmit into a turn nobody you want is listening to.

Color code (0–15). A digital "key" for the repeater — the equivalent of the PL/CTCSS tone on analog. Your color code must match the repeater's or it ignores you. It stops a second nearby system on the same frequency from interfering.4

Talkgroup. A numeric ID for a virtual group of users. Your radio only un-mutes for the talkgroups it's set to hear. This is the layer that decides who is in the conversation, independent of frequency.3

The mental model Frequency + Time slot = the physical pipe (which carrier, which turn). Color code = the lock on that pipe. Talkgroup = the address label on the voice flowing through it.
Why this matters for our bridge Our DMR↔PoC bridge sits at the network end of this pipe. To route a DMR transmission to the right PoC group, it must read the talkgroup (the "who") — and to send voice back the other way it must stamp outgoing audio with the correct talkgroup, time slot, and color code for the target repeater. All four fields are routing metadata our product has to carry, not just the audio. The talkgroup is the field we'll most likely map onto a PoC group.
