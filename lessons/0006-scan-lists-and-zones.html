DMR Radio Basics Lesson 06

Scan, Scan Lists & Zones

How one radio listens to many things, and how hundreds of channels stay organised. The operational layer your bridge may need to emulate.

The previous lessons covered how DMR works. This one covers how it's operated — the everyday ergonomics of monitoring several talkgroups and managing a crowded radio. These are code plug constructs, not over-the-air features, but your PoC side will likely need equivalents so a bridged user behaves the way a DMR user expects.

Zones — organising the channel knob

A radio can hold hundreds of channels. A zone is a named bucket of channels — typically grouped by location or purpose ("Home Repeaters", "Travel", "Site B") — so the operator switches context instead of scrolling endlessly.1 Selecting a zone re-populates the channel knob with just that zone's channels.

Scan lists — listening to many at once

Normally a radio hears only its selected channel's traffic. A scan list is a set of channels the radio steps through, stopping on whichever has activity — so one operator can monitor several talkgroups/slots without manually switching.2

With auto-scan, selecting a channel that has a scan list attached starts scanning automatically; the radio lands on active traffic and resumes scanning when it clears.2 Many radios also support a priority channel that's checked more often so important traffic isn't missed.

The distinction that trips people up A zone organises what you can manually select. A scan list controls what the radio monitors automatically. Different jobs: one is filing, the other is listening.
For the bridge A DMR user who scans expects to hear any of several talkgroups and to have one active at a time. To make a bridged PoC user feel native:
  • Scan ⇄ multi-group monitoring: a PoC user mapped to a scanned set should receive all those groups, surfacing whichever is active — mirror the "stop on activity" behaviour.
  • One talker at a time: a DMR slot carries one conversation; decide how your PoC side arbitrates if two mapped groups talk at once (priority? queue?).
  • Zones are pure UI/organisation — they likely need no bridge logic, just a clean mapping of which channels (and thus talkgroups) are reachable.
