DMR Radio Basics Lesson 13

Emergency & Panic

The orange button. It's two mechanisms wearing one name — a data alarm and a flagged voice call — and your bridge must catch both, on the right channel, and elevate them.

That dedicated emergency button puts the radio into Emergency mode and signals distress to a dispatcher or talkgroup. For a safety-relevant product this is the highest-stakes feature to bridge correctly: miss it, mis-route it, or let it get stuck, and someone in trouble goes unheard.

Two mechanisms, one button

Everything reduces to these two — the rest are combinations:1

MechanismWhat it isHow the bridge detects it
Emergency AlarmA data/signalling message (CSBK / short data), no voice — source ID → target group, sent confirmed with retries until ACKParse the inbound emergency CSBK / short-data PDU
Emergency VoiceA normal voice call carrying an emergency flag in the Link Control (FLCO)Inspect the LC/FLCO of the voice header & embedded LC

The common combinations

  • Alarm only — signalling, repeated until acknowledged, then it stops.1
  • Alarm + Call — alarm first, then follow-up PTT becomes an emergency-flagged voice call.
  • Alarm + Voice to follow (Hot Mic) — alarm, then the radio auto-keys the mic (no PTT) so dispatch hears the scene. Vendor-configurable duration.
  • Silent — emergency raised with all local indications suppressed (covert duress).
Standard vs vendor — be precise ETSI (TS 102 361-1/-2) defines the wire representation: the emergency flag in the voice Link Control / FLCO, the control-signalling emergency message, and the call-priority concept. Everything else — Man Down (tilt/no-motion sensor), Lone Worker (inactivity timer), Hot Mic duration, Emergency Revert, Silent, and priority-1 preemption rules — are vendor features built on top. Those triggers only start an emergency; once started it rides the standard alarm/voice path.2

Emergency Revert — the one that trips up integrators

A radio can be programmed to send the emergency on a designated revert channel/talkgroup regardless of where the user currently is — then return.3 So the emergency may appear on a talkgroup the user wasn't even using. (Note: some modes, e.g. Capacity Plus, don't support revert at all.)

Emergency can preempt a busy channel — in trunked systems it's auto-allocated priority 1; on conventional channels an "impolite" retry transmits over existing activity.1

What this means for your bridge — the high-stakes one
  • Detect on BOTH paths: the emergency alarm (a CSBK with no voice) and the emergency flag in voice LC/FLCO (hot-mic and alarm-and-call carry it in voice). Watch both or you'll miss cases.
  • Monitor the revert talkgroup, not just the user's nominal one — or the emergency never reaches you.
  • Elevate to a PoC emergency state: force-pop it to the PoC dispatcher and preempt normal traffic to mirror priority-1; queuing it behind ordinary calls defeats the purpose.
  • Handle ACK & clear bidirectionally: decide whether the bridge/PoC dispatcher ACKs the alarm (to stop the radio's retry loop), and reliably propagate clear — a stuck emergency state is a real hazard.
  • Don't over-trust it: Radio IDs aren't authenticated, so a spoofed emergency is possible — consider source allowlists. De-dupe repeated alarm PDUs. And respect silent emergencies — don't send loud confirmations back that blow a user's cover.
  • SDK: dispatch-grade APIs (Motorola network API, Hytera HDAP) usually expose emergency as a discrete event; raw RF/MMDVM means parsing the CSBK and LC flag yourself. Confirm for your module.
