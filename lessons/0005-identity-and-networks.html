DMR Radio Basics Lesson 05

Identity & Networks

Who is this radio, and what infrastructure carries it? The two questions that tell your bridge whom to map and which DMR world it lives in.

You can now read a channel, follow a transmission, route by talkgroup, and reason about the codec. The last foundation is identity and infrastructure: how a specific radio is named, and the spectrum of networks — from a single hotspot to worldwide systems — that your PoC platform stands in for.

Radio ID — the name of a radio

Every DMR radio carries a unique Radio ID (DMR ID), a number issued centrally by RadioID.net — the single source since 2018, used across Brandmeister, DMR-MARC and DMR+ alike.1 It's what a Private Call is addressed to, and what shows up as "who is talking" on the receiving radio. A hotspot gets the operator's 7-digit ID plus two digits (e.g. 1234567 01).2

For the bridge The Radio ID is your join key. Just as talkgroups map to PoC groups, Radio IDs map to PoC users/identities. Keep a table: DMR ID ⇄ PoC user. You'll need it for Private Calls and for showing "who's speaking" correctly on the PoC side.

The three Tiers — which DMR world you're in

DMR ships in three profiles. Knowing which one your deployment targets sets your expectations for repeaters, slots and trunking.3

TierWhat it isRelevance to us
Tier ILicence-free, low power, direct/simplex only — no repeatersEdge case: radio-to-radio with no infra
Tier IILicensed conventional 2-slot repeaters; group/private calls, text, encryptionThe common case — design for this
Tier IIITrunked, multi-site, dynamic channel allocation, roamingOut of scope for v1; note the exception exists

The infrastructure ladder

From smallest to largest, the things that carry DMR beyond a single radio:

Hotspotpersonal, 1 radio
→
Repeaterlocal/regional
→
NetworkBrandmeister / MARC
→
Reflectortalkgroup meeting room
  • Hotspot — a low-power personal device (Pi-Star/MMDVM) linking one radio to a network over the internet; a repeater shrunk to desk size.4
  • Network — internet master servers (Brandmeister, DMR-MARC, DMR+) that route talkgroups between all connected repeaters and hotspots.2
  • Reflector — a "room" radios/repeaters connect to so everyone linked hears everyone; an alternative way to group traffic alongside talkgroups.
Where your product sits Recall from Lesson 2: your on-device bridge replaces the network rung of this ladder with your PoC platform. You're not joining Brandmeister — you're the alternative transport. That means you also own the equivalents of its identity registry (Radio ID ⇄ PoC user) and its routing table (talkgroup ⇄ PoC group). Design for Tier II behaviour and you cover the vast majority of real deployments.
