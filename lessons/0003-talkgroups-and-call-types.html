DMR Radio Basics Lesson 03

Talkgroups & Call Types

The talkgroup is the address on the voice. Master how DMR decides who hears whom, and the DMR↔PoC group mapping designs itself.

In Lesson 2 you saw that a network routes voice by talkgroup. This lesson opens that idea up: what a talkgroup actually is, the three call types, and how a radio decides what to un-mute for. This is the layer your bridge translates into PoC groups.

Talkgroup = a virtual address, not a frequency

A talkgroup is just a number (e.g. TG 91 "Worldwide", TG 3100 "US Nationwide", a club's TG 235) that names a group of users.1 Voice tagged with a talkgroup reaches every radio currently listening for that number — regardless of which repeater or frequency they're on. Frequency is the pipe; the talkgroup is the address label on what flows through it.

The three call types

Every DMR transmission is addressed as one of three call types:2

Who a transmission addresses
Group Call→ everyone listening to a talkgroup. Many-to-many. The everyday case.
Private Call→ one specific Radio ID. One-to-one, like a direct call.
All Call→ everyone on the system, one-way. Rare; mostly commercial dispatch.

Group Calls are addressed by talkgroup number; Private Calls by an individual's Radio ID (the unique per-radio number from RadioID.net).3 So "who receives this" is answered by either a talkgroup (a group) or a Radio ID (a person).

How a radio decides what to un-mute

A radio doesn't hear every talkgroup on a slot — only the ones it's set to. Two mechanisms:1

  • Static talkgroups — permanently subscribed in the radio's receive group list (and often kept "always on" at the repeater). Always audible.
  • Dynamic talkgroups — temporarily activated by transmitting to one; the network routes it to you for a short timeout, then drops it.
Putting it together A transmission carries: call type + destination (talkgroup number for Group, Radio ID for Private) + the channel's time slot and color code. A radio plays it only if that destination is in its receive list on that slot.

Mapping DMR talkgroups onto your PoC groups

Design decisions this forces
  • Group calls map naturally: each DMR talkgroup ⇄ one PoC group. A lookup table you control.
  • Private calls need an identity map: DMR Radio ID ⇄ a PoC user. Decide if v1 supports them at all.
  • Direction matters: PoC→DMR must stamp outgoing voice with the right talkgroup, slot and color code for the target repeater — the PoC side carries none of those natively, so your map must supply them.
  • Static vs dynamic: decide whether a PoC group is always bridged to its talkgroup, or only activated on demand (mirrors DMR's static/dynamic behaviour).
