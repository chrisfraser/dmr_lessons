DMR Radio Basics Lesson 11

Analog vs Digital Channels

Most DMR radios are dual-mode — they do old analog FM too. The gap between the two modes is widest exactly where your bridge cares most: metadata.

A DMR radio's codeplug can hold both analog FM channels and digital DMR channels side by side, selected per channel.1 Knowing how they differ tells you what your bridge gets handed when a transmission arrives — and analog hands you far less than DMR.

Two differences worth feeling, not just reading

The "digital cliff"

Analog degrades gracefully — as you move away, hiss rises but speech stays partly intelligible far into the fringe. DMR uses FEC to deliver clean audio right up to a threshold, then falls off a cliff: below it, decoding fails and you get silence, not static.2 DMR is cleaner for most of the range; analog can claw out the last desperate mile. (Be skeptical of "DMR has longer range" marketing — usable range is similar.)

Squelch vs routing

A CTCSS tone (a "PL tone") only decides whether your speaker un-mutes — it says nothing about who's talking. DMR's color code is the nearest analog to that filter, but DMR adds a whole second dimension: the talkgroup + time slot decide routing — who actually hears whom.3

Mixed mode & migration

A dynamic mixed-mode repeater auto-senses each incoming signal and repeats it in kind — analog call repeated as analog, DMR as digital — letting old and new radios share one channel during a fleet's transition off analog.4 (In practice such repeaters usually only repeat tone-qualified analog, so open chatter doesn't block DMR access.)

Why this is the lesson that matters for your bridge The split is about metadata. When you bridge a DMR call you get rich, structured routing for free — source Radio ID → PoC user, talkgroup → PoC group, call type → 1:1 vs group (everything from Lessons 1&3). When you bridge an analog channel you get audio and almost nothing else: a matched tone tells you "someone is talking," not who or for whom. Any analog→PoC mapping must be inferred from which physical channel/tone the audio arrived on, or bolted on with extra signalling (DTMF, MDC-1200). Budget real design effort for analog identity; DMR gives it to you. If your product only needs to bridge DMR, you dodge this entirely.
